Uniqueness & Limitations of Oral History as a New-Born Research Approach

About IDOH

Our full name is Industrial Dongguan: An Oral Herstory. Initiated by Tsinglan high school students, this project aims to conduct in-depth interviews with female migrant workers in Dongguan, record and disseminate their personal stories. We hope to promote the society’s understanding of how the fate of individuals contribute to the modern industrial world, thus forming a deeper, more inclusive connection among people.

I remember hearing this somewhere: when an old man dies, a library burns down… I wished there was a place you could go, and sit in a room like a movie theater and look through a catalogue of a zillion names and then you could press a button and a face would appear on the screen to tell you about the life that had been. It would be a living memorial to the generations who had gone on before, and you could hear their voices though those voices had been stilled for a hundred years. It seemed to me, as I walked in the presence of all those stilled voices that would never be heard again, that we were a wasteful breed. We had thrown away the past, and our future was impoverished for it.Robert McCammon, "Boy’s Life"

In simple words, oral history is to record the original conversations of the interviewees with researchers, using modern technology, such as transcripts, audio recordings, and video recordings, and to compile the information into a written script after verifying them with literature sources. As an important source of information about society and history, oral history happens every day in very natural forms: the rambling reminiscences of the past from the elderly to the youngest in the family, the passing down of skills from a craftsman to an apprentice... Each of us can do oral history or be the subject of oral history, it’s just that most of the time, we do not realize the significance of recording and disseminating oral history. Oral histories are not only historical references for future generations, but also an opportunity for individuals to reflect on their life experiences, listen to their inner voices, and carry the emotions of their relatives, which can foster a sense of unity within a community.

Limitations and Challenges

1. The unreliability of memory -Memory could decline due to ageing or illness. -There might be deliberate distortion of memory according to personal emotions, such as instinctive self-glorification, self-defense, and self-perception dissonance. Solution: Ask questions about specific details, such as dates, people, places, events, and skills, related to the interviewee's life; the questions should shift from general to specific. Access diaries, letters, past works, public documents, and call consulting informants, old friends, and family members to recall together. However, as with any historical research method, oral history can never restore the most authentic history. We can see bias itself as another kind of historical truth, because it conveys people’s true perceptions of the world.

2. The interviewee is not used to or does not like the colloquial language and cannot help but make changes to the manuscript after the editor sends it to the interviewee. Solution: Try to communicate with the interviewee and try to maintain a spoken form. If the interviewee cannot be dissuaded, make a note on the written revision that a certain part is a “textual patch” added by the interviewee.

3. The completeness of the interviewee’s statement does not meet his or her expectations, so he or she spends a long time before each interview revising or adding to the previous interview.
Solution: -When the additional content is difficult to categorize by topic, has a large volume, and discusses diverse issues, make it a a separate article. -When the supplemental material’s subject matter is consistent, new, and the previous statement on the subject is not particularly complete, the content of the supplemental narrative could be grouped into a specific topic that was previously spoken about, and a special note should be made to remind the readers. -Delete additions that have little new information and are likely to cause logical disorders.

4. The interviewee appears to make associations and crosses between different events and people.
Solutions:
1. Remind the interviewee that he or she is off topic. 2. Let the interviewee speak to a certain extend and avoid interrupting him/her or affecting his/her enthusiasm for expressing. If the topic is not off by too far, then keep it as it is. If the topic is off by too far and can become a separate chapter, put this part into a specific, existing topic. If the topic is off by too far, is not very meaningful, and the message is unclear, then delete it.
